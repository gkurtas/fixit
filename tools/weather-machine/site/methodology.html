How this works — methodology and sources.

This page shows National Weather Service alerts and on-the-ground storm reports for the Philadelphia region in close to real time, and rewrites the official, jargon-filled wording into plain language so it's faster to read in a hurry. It is an independent project and is not an official National Weather Service product — always follow official NWS and local emergency guidance.

What you're looking at

The left column is a live feed: active watches, warnings and advisories for the NWS Mount Holly office, followed by local storm reports (hail, wind, flooding, tornado sightings) from the past 24 hours. The right side is a map of the same information, with an optional precipitation radar overlay.

Each alert and report shows a short plain-language summary. The exact, official National Weather Service wording is always one tap away under “Show official NWS text.”

Where the information comes from

Everything here is built from free, public, official or well-established sources:

  • Alerts — the National Weather Service alerts feed (api.weather.gov), for the Mount Holly / Philadelphia (PHI) forecast office.
  • Storm reports — the Iowa Environmental Mesonet (IEM), which collects the NWS local storm reports.
  • Watch and zone areas — official NWS county and zone boundaries, used to draw watch areas on the map.
  • Radar — RainViewer, a precipitation radar service.
  • Base map — OpenStreetMap.

The page refreshes every few minutes, so the information tracks the official sources closely. Alerts drop off the moment they expire, and storm reports age out after 24 hours.

How the plain-language summaries are written

Official alerts are written for precision, not speed-reading — they're full of abbreviations (“TSTM WND GST”), capitalized headers and technical phrasing. We use an artificial-intelligence model (Anthropic's Claude) to rewrite that wording into plain English.

The single most important rule: the rewrite must not change the facts. Here's how that's enforced:

  • Only the wording is simplified. The location, the times, the issuing office, the severity and any measurements are taken straight from the official data and shown unchanged — the model never gets to alter them.
  • Numbers are protected. Every number, measurement, time and place in a summary must already appear in the original. An automatic check rejects any summary that introduces a figure that wasn't there (a made-up wind speed or hail size), and in that case the official text is shown instead.
  • The official text is always available. Every card links to the exact National Weather Service wording.
  • AI summaries are labeled. Anything rewritten by the model is marked “AI-simplified.” The official event name (for example, “Tornado Warning”) is never changed.

AI can still make mistakes. The summaries are a convenience to help you read faster — they are not the official record. For anything that affects your safety, read the official NWS text and follow official guidance.

What's on the map

  • Warning polygons — the precise areas under a storm-based warning (tornado, severe thunderstorm, flash flood), shown as solid, colored shapes. These come from the Mount Holly office and four neighboring offices, so you can see severe weather just outside the immediate area too. Tap a shape to see which office issued it.
  • Watch areas — the broader “be prepared” zones, drawn as faint dashed outlines beneath the warnings, so the urgent “happening now” warnings always stand out.
  • Radar — a precipitation overlay you can turn on or off with the “Radar” button; your choice is remembered.
  • Storm-report dots — points where something was actually observed or measured.

What the colors mean

Alerts are colored by severity, a rating the National Weather Service assigns (we don't decide it):

  • Extreme (purple) — extraordinary threat to life or property.
  • Severe (red) — significant threat to life or property.
  • Moderate (amber) — possible threat to life or property.
  • Minor (blue) — minimal to no known threat.

The palette is chosen to stay distinguishable for colorblind readers and to meet contrast standards.

The area we cover

The text feed covers the National Weather Service Mount Holly, NJ office, whose forecast area includes Philadelphia, the surrounding Pennsylvania counties, much of New Jersey, Delaware and part of Maryland. The map widens that view to also show warning polygons and watches from the neighboring New York, State College, Binghamton and Baltimore/Washington offices.

Important limits

  • This is for situational awareness only. It is not an official source and not a substitute for NWS alerts or local emergency instructions.
  • Not every alert appears as a shape on the map. Short-fuse warnings carry a precise polygon; many advisories are issued for whole zones and appear in the feed list rather than as a drawn area.
  • Radar is an estimate of precipitation, not ground truth, and can lag or contain artifacts.
  • Data can occasionally be delayed or incomplete if an upstream source is slow.

Why this exists

Official weather alerts are accurate but hard to read quickly, especially on a phone during fast-moving weather. The goal here is to make that information clearer and faster to scan for the public — without changing what it says — while keeping the official wording always within reach.
